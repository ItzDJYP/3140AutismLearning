Understanding Autism

Overview

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a lifelong neurodevelopmental condition that affects how a child thinks, communicates, and interacts with their world. It usually becomes noticeable in early childhood. Children with autism process information, routines, sensory input, and social cues in unique ways. While many enjoy predictability or have focused interests, others may struggle with communication or sensory experiences.

Today, approximately 1 in 31 children in the U.S. is diagnosed with autism, and globally, about 1 in 100 children. These figures reflect better awareness, enhanced screening, and broader diagnostic standards—autism isn’t more common, but we’re better at identifying it. Kids of all backgrounds and identities can be autistic, and although diagnosed more often in boys, girls may be under-identified.

Levels of Support

When diagnosing autism, clinicians describe how much help a child needs right now using three levels. These levels help guide the type and amount of support each child receives, and they may change over time.

  • Level 1 (Requiring Support): Everyday independence is possible with some help—for instance, with social interactions, flexibility, or planning.
  • Level 2 (Requiring Substantial Support): More noticeable challenges with communication and behavior; needs consistent support across settings.
  • Level 3 (Requiring Very Substantial Support): Significant daily challenges with communication, routines, and behavior—intensive help is needed most of the day.

This level system replaces older “high-functioning” or “low-functioning” labels, which can be misleading. It emphasizes each child’s current needs rather than generalizations.

Signs & Screening

Between ages 4–6, children’s social and communication skills become more complex. Watching how your child plays and interacts can reveal subtle signs of autism that may emerge in preschool years.

  • Social interaction: Prefers playing alone, avoids imaginative or cooperative play, doesn’t respond when name is called, limited eye contact, doesn’t share interest or emotions with others (like showing toys or pointing things out).
  • Communication & language: Has difficulty forming sentences, uses repetitive phrases, reverses pronouns (“you” instead of “I”), speaks in flat or singsong voice, struggles with gestures or understanding jokes/sarcasm.
  • Anxiety around change or routines: Becomes upset at minor changes (like a different snack), insists on sameness, shows distress over small transitions.
  • Repetitive behaviors or narrow interests: Lines up toys or objects in exact order, repeats certain actions, intensely focused on specific topics.
  • Sensory differences: Reacts strongly to sounds, textures, or lights—may cover ears, dislike wearing certain clothes, or avoid messy play.

Red Flags in Everyday Milestones

  • Still not using sentences consistently, or using very few words by age 4.
  • Rarely plays pretend—like pretending to cook, talk on toy phones, or acting out stories.
  • Doesn’t seek comfort from familiar adults—or seems “tuned out” of social interactions.

Screening Tools for Ages 4–6

For preschoolers and early school-age children, tools like the CAST (Childhood Autism Spectrum Test) and the SCQ (Social Communication Questionnaire) help identify communication and social patterns typical in this age group.

  • The CAST is a parent-completed questionnaire for kids aged 4–11. It measures social, communication, play, and repetitive behaviors in about 5–10 minutes. A higher score may indicate the need for a full evaluation.
  • The SCQ is another parent-based tool for ages 4 and older, focusing on recent behaviors and communication skills.

Trust your instincts—if you notice these signs, bring them up at your child’s next pediatric visit. Early screening and evaluation can lead to better-tailored support and brighter outcomes.

Diagnosis

When autism isn't caught early, it often becomes clearer during preschool years. Around ages 4–6, parents and teachers may notice ongoing differences in how a child interacts with others, plays, speaks, or adapts to change.

A formal diagnosis involves a team of trusted professionals—like developmental pediatricians, child psychologists, speech-language therapists, and sometimes occupational therapists. The evaluation is a careful process, using multiple tools and observations to understand your child's unique strengths and challenges.

What to Expect During Evaluation

  • Caregiver interview: You may be asked about your child’s development milestones, language and play history, behaviors, and how they handle daily routines or changes.
  • Direct observation: Clinicians often use structured tools like the ADOS-2, where they play with your child and look for signs of social communication differences, imaginative play, and repetitive behaviors.
  • Rating scales: Tools like CARS (Childhood Autism Rating Scale) help professionals rate behavior on traits like emotional response, adaptation to change, and communication.
  • Screening tools review: Questionnaires like the CAST or SCQ—completed by you—provide snapshots of social, communication, and imaginative play behaviors typical at this age.
  • Developmental testing: Your child may be evaluated for language, thinking, and daily living skills to understand cognitive strengths and areas needing support.
  • Medical checks as needed: Often includes hearing tests, and in some cases, genetic testing to identify any underlying genetic conditions.

Why a Thorough Evaluation Matters

Accurate diagnosis by a skilled team ensures the support matches your child’s unique needs—whether that’s school planning, daily routines at home, or therapies that promote social and language skills. Many children receive a reliable diagnosis around ages 4–5 when signs become clearer, so it’s perfectly valid to seek evaluation even if earlier screenings were inconclusive.

Interventions

Early help matters. Research shows that intervention begun in early childhood—including behavioral, speech, and occupational therapies—can significantly improve communication, social skills, and daily routines.

  • Behavioral Therapy: Supports positive behaviors and social skills through structured and play-based methods.
  • Speech-Language Therapy: Helps children communicate—whether by words, gestures, or picture-based systems.
  • Occupational Therapy: Supports sensory regulation, motor skills, self-care, and daily routines.
  • Early Intervention Programs: Collaborative services that support both child and family, ideally beginning in toddler years.

Every child is unique—so therapies should be individualized, respectful, and empowering, aiming to help children learn, participate, and build independence at home, school, and beyond.

Myths & Facts

  1. Myth: Vaccines cause autism.
    Fact: Multiple large, peer-reviewed studies—across different countries and involving millions of children—have found no connection between vaccines (including the MMR vaccine and those with thimerosal) and autism. The original study that claimed a link was found to be fraudulent and was retracted.
  2. Myth: Autism is a mental illness or a disease.
    Fact: Autism is a neurodevelopmental difference—it’s part of how a child’s brain works, not a disease to be cured.
  3. Myth: All autistic children are the same or have special talents like being geniuses.
    Fact: Autism is a wide spectrum. Some children may have intense interests or excel in certain areas, but others may struggle with language or social skills. Every child’s profile is unique.
  4. Myth: Only boys are autistic.
    Fact: Autistic individuals include all genders. Girls, in particular, may “mask” their differences and are often diagnosed later than boys.
  5. Myth: Autism can be cured.
    Fact: Autism is lifelong and not something to cure. However, early supports—like therapies focused on communication, play, and daily living skills—can help children thrive.
  6. Myth: Autistic children don’t want friends or emotional connection.
    Fact: Many autistic children want friendships, but they may interact or show emotions differently—through routines, play, or their own way of expressing feeling.

What Is Autism? (For Kids)

Autism means your brain works in a special way that makes you, you! Here’s what that can mean:

  • Some sounds, lights, or textures might feel extra strong or different—and that’s okay.
  • You might love the same routine, like playing the same game or line of toys. That’s your special interest!
  • You might talk, move, or play in ways that are different—and that makes you unique.

Being autistic means seeing the world differently—and that’s one of the many wonderful ways there are to be.
